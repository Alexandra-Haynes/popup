My deployment in Afghanistan

7 months in Kandahar
Before the deployment, I would never have imagined that in 2021 there would still be people living in mud huts or rag tents, who don't even have water, parents who leave home in search of work for less than a dollar a day and never return because they are simply in the wrong place at the wrong time.

I didn't know there were still places in this world where women are walking sheets. At meetings, we heard of soldiers freezing to death on post, soldiers asking for help to send a helicopter to drop bread from the helicopter, because they knew they couldn't land because the Taliban had surrounded and blocked the area. Soldiers who were left with their colleagues killed on post for days because they couldn't get to them to evacuate them, or with the desperate cries of wounded colleagues who couldn't be helped in any way. And yet some soldiers were not paid by the month even though their pay is very, very low. I won't talk about corruption, but I choose to talk about true patriotism, about fighting with one's life for a better future, which has not come from so many generations of soldiers in one's family, about putting oneself as a pawn on a chessboard with pieces of many colours, not just black and white.

Every day is a fight for survival and never is the night safer. Beyond the war, roads littered with explosive devices, rockets and terrorists, Afghans also struggle with hunger, poverty, disease, cold and heat. My phone rings every morning with more images of terrorists killed in air strikes or suicide bombers. I stared at those violent photos for minutes, stuck on the smile that would forever be plastered on their faces, convinced that they had died doing the right thing. Over time, we all forget that Afghanistan exists. And not just us, but the whole planet seems blind to this parallel world to the world we know, with these people guilty of being born into war...

I conclude my experience by saying just this: I would be afraid to be born again.

March 2021
